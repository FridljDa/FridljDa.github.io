I have a mathematics background, and kernel regression is something I have studied deeply as part of advanced statistics.

Self-attention, on the other hand, felt slippery to me when I started learning it. It is confusing that the input tokens $x$ play so many roles at once: they are queries, keys, and values.

Based on §15.4.2 and §15.5 of Kevin P. Murphy's *[Probabilistic Machine Learning: An Introduction](https://probml.github.io/pml-book/book1.html)* I will derive the self-attention from kernel regression.


---

## 1. The setup: kernel regression

Suppose you have $n$ training pairs $(x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)$ and a new test point $x$. You want to predict $f(x)$.

The kernel regression estimator (Nadaraya-Watson) says: take a **weighted average of all the training labels**, where the weight given to $y_i$ depends on how similar $x$ is to $x_i$:

$$
f(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \omega_i(x,\, x_{1:n})\, y_i
\tag{1}
$$

where the weights $\omega_i$ are non-negative and sum to 1. This is already a familiar structure: a convex combination of values, with data-dependent weights. The only question is how to define "similarity."

---

## 2. Choosing a kernel

The most natural similarity measure is the **Gaussian kernel**:

$$
K_\sigma(u) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{u^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
\tag{2}
$$

where $\sigma$ is the bandwidth — a smaller $\sigma$ means the model only looks at very nearby points, a larger $\sigma$ means it smooths broadly.

We define the attention score of $x$ toward $x_i$ as:

$$
a(x, x_i) = K_\sigma(x - x_i)
\tag{3}
$$

To normalize these into weights, we divide by the sum over all training points. The normalizing constant $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}$ cancels in numerator and denominator, so we can drop it.

---

## 3. Reparametrizing: from $\sigma$ to $\alpha$

Let's reparametrize by setting $\alpha^2 = 1/\sigma^2$ (i.e., $\alpha$ is the inverse bandwidth, or *precision*). The kernel becomes:

$$
K(u;\, \alpha) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{\alpha^2}{2}\,u^2\right)
\tag{4}
$$

This is just a scaling convention, but it will cleanly match the form of attention scores we see in practice.

Plugging (4) into the Nadaraya-Watson estimator (1):

$$
f(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\exp\!\left[-\dfrac{1}{2}((x - x_i)\,\alpha)^2\right]}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{n} \exp\!\left[-\dfrac{1}{2}((x - x_j)\,\alpha)^2\right]}\, y_i
\tag{5}
$$

---

## 4. Recognizing the softmax

Look at the weights in (5). For a fixed $x$, define the vector of scores:

$$
s_i(x) = -\frac{1}{2}\left((x - x_i)\,\alpha\right)^2, \quad i = 1, \ldots, n
\tag{6}
$$

Then the weight on $y_i$ is exactly:

$$
\omega_i = \frac{e^{s_i}}{\sum_j e^{s_j}} = \operatorname{softmax}_i(s_1, \ldots, s_n)
\tag{7}
$$

So (5) can be written compactly as:

$$
f(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \operatorname{softmax}_i\!\left(s_1(x), \ldots, s_n(x)\right)\, y_i
\tag{8}
$$

The softmax is not a design choice imposed from outside — it **falls out automatically** from normalizing a Gaussian kernel. The kernel ensures the weights are positive; normalization makes them sum to one; together they give you softmax.

---

## 5. Naming the pieces: queries, keys, values

Now rename the objects to match the standard attention vocabulary:

| Kernel regression | Attention |
|---|---|
| Test point $x$ | **Query** $q$ |
| Training inputs $x_i$ | **Keys** $k_i$ |
| Training labels $y_i$ | **Values** $v_i$ |
| Attention score $s_i(x)$ | $a(q, k_i)$ |

With this naming, (8) becomes:

$$
\operatorname{Attn}(q,\, \{(k_i, v_i)\}) = \sum_i \operatorname{softmax}_i\!\left(a(q, k_1), \ldots, a(q, k_n)\right)\, v_i
\tag{9}
$$

This is the general attention formula. We have derived it, not postulated it.

---

## 6. From Gaussian scores to dot-product scores

The Gaussian score (6) involves the squared distance $(q - k_i)^2$. Let's expand it:

$$
-\frac{\alpha^2}{2}(q - k_i)^2 = -\frac{\alpha^2}{2}\|q\|^2 + \alpha^2\, q^\top k_i - \frac{\alpha^2}{2}\|k_i\|^2
\tag{10}
$$

The first term $-\frac{\alpha^2}{2}\|q\|^2$ is constant across all $i$, so it vanishes inside the softmax. If we additionally assume all keys are normalized so $\|k_i\| = 1$, the third term is also constant and disappears too.

What remains is:

$$
a(q, k_i) = \alpha^2\, q^\top k_i
\tag{11}
$$

This is the **dot-product attention score**. The $\alpha^2$ factor is just a scaling constant — in practice it is set to $1/\sqrt{d}$ where $d$ is the dimension of the query/key vectors (this keeps the magnitudes of the dot products stable as $d$ grows). Plugging (11) into (9):

$$
\operatorname{Attn}(q, K, V) = \operatorname{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right)V
\tag{12}
$$

where $Q$, $K$, $V$ are the matrices of queries, keys, and values stacked into rows. This is **scaled dot-product attention** — the exact formula in "Attention Is All You Need" [Vaswani et al., 2017].

---

## 7. Making it parametric: learned projections

So far, queries, keys, and values are the raw inputs. But in a neural network we want to *learn* what aspects of the input to compare. We introduce learned linear projections:

$$
q = W^{(q)} x_{\text{query}}, \quad k_i = W^{(k)} x_i, \quad v_i = W^{(v)} x_i
\tag{13}
$$

This is the step that turns nonparametric kernel regression into a *trained* attention mechanism. The matrices $W^{(q)}, W^{(k)}, W^{(v)}$ are learned end-to-end by backpropagation.

---

## 8. Self-attention

The final step is the key trick of the Transformer: make the model attend **to itself**.

In standard sequence-to-sequence attention, a decoder state queries the encoder's representations. In **self-attention**, we let every position in a sequence attend to every other position *in the same sequence*. For a sequence of input tokens $x_1, \ldots, x_n$:

$$
y_i = \operatorname{Attn}\!\left(W^{(q)} x_i,\;\{(W^{(k)} x_j,\; W^{(v)} x_j)\}_{j=1}^{n}\right)
\tag{14}
$$

Each token $x_i$ acts simultaneously as a query (asking "who is relevant to me?") and contributes a key and value (answering "here is what I offer to others"). This lets the model build **context-dependent representations**: the embedding of the word "it" can depend on whether the surrounding sentence is about an animal or a street.

Because there are no recurrences, all $n$ outputs $y_1, \ldots, y_n$ can be computed in parallel — this is what makes Transformers so much faster to train than RNNs.

---

## Summary

Here is the full derivation in one view:

$$
\underbrace{f(x) = \sum_i \omega_i(x)\, y_i}_{\text{kernel regression}}
\;\xrightarrow{\text{Gaussian kernel}}\;
\underbrace{\sum_i \operatorname{softmax}_i\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}\|(q - k_i)\alpha\|^2\right) v_i}_{\text{nonparametric attention}}
$$

$$
\xrightarrow{\text{expand, drop constants}}\;
\underbrace{\operatorname{softmax}\!\left(\tfrac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right)V}_{\text{scaled dot-product attention}}
\;\xrightarrow{\text{add projections + self-query}}\;
\underbrace{y_i = \operatorname{Attn}(W^{(q)}x_i, W^{(k)}X, W^{(v)}X)}_{\text{self-attention}}
$$

Self-attention is not a mysterious black box. It is the natural result of applying a Gaussian similarity kernel, normalizing with softmax, adding learned projections, and letting a sequence attend to itself. Every piece has a reason.
