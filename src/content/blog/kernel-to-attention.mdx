I have a mathematics background, and kernel regression is something I have studied deeply as part of advanced statistics.

Self-attention, on the other hand, felt slippery to me when I started learning it. It is confusing that the input tokens $\boldsymbol{x}$ play so many roles at once: they are queries, keys, and values.

If you happen to have the same background — comfortable with kernel regression, uncertain about attention — this post is for you. Following §15.4.2 and §15.5 of Kevin P. Murphy's *[Probabilistic Machine Learning: An Introduction](https://probml.github.io/pml-book/book1.html)*, I will derive self-attention from kernel regression step by step. I keep Murphy's notation where possible so the post can be read alongside the book.

---

## 1. Kernel regression

Suppose we have $n$ training pairs $(\boldsymbol{x}_1, y_1), \ldots, (\boldsymbol{x}_n, y_n)$ with $\boldsymbol{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and $y_i \in \mathbb{R}$, and we want to predict $y$ at a new test point $\boldsymbol{x}$. The **Nadaraya–Watson** estimator says: take a weighted average of the training labels, where the weight given to $y_i$ depends on how similar $\boldsymbol{x}$ is to $\boldsymbol{x}_i$:

$$
f(\boldsymbol{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_i(\boldsymbol{x},\, \boldsymbol{x}_{1:n})\, y_i
\tag{1}
$$

Here $\alpha_i(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_{1:n}) \geq 0$ are weights that sum to one (for any fixed $\boldsymbol{x}$). A standard way to build them is to start from a **density kernel**. We will use the Gaussian:

$$
\mathcal{K}_\sigma(u) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\, \exp\!\left(-\frac{u^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
\tag{2}
$$

where $\sigma$ is called the *bandwidth*. We build the weights $\alpha_i$ by normalizing the kernel across training points. Because of the normalization, the prefactor $1/\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}$ is irrelevant and we drop it. Writing $\beta^2 = 1/\sigma^2$,

$$
\alpha_i(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_{1:n}) = \frac{\exp\!\left(-\frac{\beta^2}{2}\|\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_i\|^2\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^n \exp\!\left(-\frac{\beta^2}{2}\|\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_j\|^2\right)}
\tag{3}
$$

Recall the softmax:

$$
\operatorname{softmax}_i(s_1, \ldots, s_n) = \frac{e^{s_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^n e^{s_j}}
\tag{4}
$$

Comparing (3) with (4), the weights are exactly a softmax — applied to the *exponent* of the Gaussian, not to its value. We give that exponent a name. Define the **attention score**

$$
a(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_i) = -\frac{\beta^2}{2}\, \|\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_i\|^2
\tag{5}
$$

With this, (3) becomes

$$
\alpha_i(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_{1:n}) = \operatorname{softmax}_i\!\left(a(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_1),\, \ldots,\, a(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_n)\right)
\tag{6}
$$

---

## 2. From kernel regression to a generic similarity

Substituting (6) into (1) gives

$$
f(\boldsymbol{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \operatorname{softmax}_i\!\left(a(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_1),\, \ldots,\, a(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_n)\right)\, y_i
\tag{7}
$$

This is still pure kernel regression — we have only rewritten it. Now observe that the Gaussian score in (5) plays *one specific role* here: it measures how similar $\boldsymbol{x}$ is to each $\boldsymbol{x}_i$. Nothing in the structure of (7) forces that particular choice. Letting $a(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_i)$ refer to a generic similarity score — not just the Gaussian one — gives

$$
f(\boldsymbol{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \operatorname{softmax}_i\!\left(a(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_1),\, \ldots,\, a(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_n)\right)\, y_i
\tag{8}
$$

Equations (7) and (8) look identical — and that is the point. Only the *meaning* of $a$ has widened: in (7) it is fixed to the Gaussian form (5); in (8) it is any real-valued function. No modelling power has been added; the similarity function has become a pluggable component. That small abstraction is what opens the door to the attention vocabulary.

---

## 3. Naming the pieces: queries, keys, values

We rename the objects to match standard attention notation:

| Kernel regression       | Attention                  | Role                                |
|-------------------------|----------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Test point $\boldsymbol{x}$      | **Query** $\boldsymbol{q}$         | What we are predicting at           |
| Training input $\boldsymbol{x}_i$ | **Key** $\boldsymbol{k}_i$         | What the query is compared against  |
| Training label $y_i$     | **Value** $\boldsymbol{v}_i$         | What gets averaged into the output  |

With this renaming, (8) becomes

$$
\operatorname{Attn}\!\left(\boldsymbol{q},\, \{(\boldsymbol{k}_i, \boldsymbol{v}_i)\}_{i=1}^n\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \operatorname{softmax}_i\!\left(a(\boldsymbol{q}, \boldsymbol{k}_1),\, \ldots,\, a(\boldsymbol{q}, \boldsymbol{k}_n)\right) \boldsymbol{v}_i
\tag{9}
$$

This is mostly a rename: $\boldsymbol{k}_i = \boldsymbol{x}_i$ and $\boldsymbol{v}_i = y_i$. The split earns its keep in §5, where learned projections will let keys and values diverge.

---

## 4. From Gaussian scores to dot-product scores

The Gaussian score in (3) involves a squared distance. Expand it:

$$
-\frac{\beta^2}{2}\, \|\boldsymbol{q} - \boldsymbol{k}_i\|^2 = -\frac{\beta^2}{2}\|\boldsymbol{q}\|^2 \;+\; \beta^2\, \boldsymbol{q}^\top \boldsymbol{k}_i \;-\; \frac{\beta^2}{2}\|\boldsymbol{k}_i\|^2
\tag{10}
$$

The first term $-\tfrac{\beta^2}{2}\|\boldsymbol{q}\|^2$ does not depend on $i$. To see why such a term vanishes, note that for any constant $c$ independent of $i$,

$$
\operatorname{softmax}_i(s_1 + c, \ldots, s_n + c) = \frac{e^{s_i + c}}{\sum_j e^{s_j + c}} = \frac{e^c\, e^{s_i}}{e^c \sum_j e^{s_j}} = \operatorname{softmax}_i(s_1, \ldots, s_n)
\tag{11}
$$

so adding an $i$-independent constant to every score leaves the weights unchanged. The $-\tfrac{\beta^2}{2}\|\boldsymbol{q}\|^2$ term is exactly such a constant and drops out. If we additionally assume keys are unit-norm, the third term $-\tfrac{\beta^2}{2}\|\boldsymbol{k}_i\|^2$ is also constant and drops out. What remains is

$$
a(\boldsymbol{q}, \boldsymbol{k}_i) = \beta^2\, \boldsymbol{q}^\top \boldsymbol{k}_i
\tag{12}
$$

This is the **dot-product attention score**. The scalar $\beta^2$ is just a constant; in practice it is set to $1/\sqrt{d}$, where $d$ is the dimension of the query and key vectors. The reason for that specific choice is statistical: if the components of $\boldsymbol{q}$ and $\boldsymbol{k}_i$ are independent with unit variance, the dot product $\boldsymbol{q}^\top \boldsymbol{k}_i$ has variance $d$. Dividing by $\sqrt{d}$ keeps the score variance $O(1)$ as $d$ grows, which prevents the softmax from saturating.

Stacking queries, keys, and values into matrices $Q$, $K$, $V$ (one row per item) and plugging (12) into (9) gives

$$
\operatorname{Attn}(Q, K, V) = \operatorname{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right) V
\tag{13}
$$

This is **scaled dot-product attention** — the exact formula from "[Attention Is All You Need](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762)" [Vaswani et al., 2017].

---

## 5. Making it parametric: learned projections

So far, queries, keys, and values are the raw inputs. But in a neural network we want to *learn* what aspects of the input to compare on and what aspects to return. Introduce three learned linear projections:

$$
\boldsymbol{q} = W^{(q)} \boldsymbol{x}_{\text{query}}, \quad \boldsymbol{k}_i = W^{(k)} \boldsymbol{x}_i, \quad \boldsymbol{v}_i = W^{(v)} \boldsymbol{x}_i
\tag{14}
$$

This is the step that turns nonparametric kernel regression into a *trained* attention mechanism. The matrices $W^{(q)}, W^{(k)}, W^{(v)}$ are learned end-to-end by backpropagation. It is also where the key/value split in §3 pays off: the same input $\boldsymbol{x}_i$ is projected through two different matrices, so the key (what a token is matched on) and value (what a token contributes) can carry genuinely different information.

---

## 6. Self-attention

The final step is the key trick of the Transformer: make the model attend **to itself**.

In standard sequence-to-sequence attention, a decoder state queries an encoder's representations. In **self-attention**, every position in a sequence attends to every other position *in the same sequence*. For input tokens $\boldsymbol{x}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{x}_n$ and each $i$,

$$
\boldsymbol{y}_i = \operatorname{Attn}\!\left(W^{(q)} \boldsymbol{x}_i,\; \left\{\left(W^{(k)} \boldsymbol{x}_j,\; W^{(v)} \boldsymbol{x}_j\right)\right\}_{j=1}^{n}\right)
\tag{15}
$$

Each token $\boldsymbol{x}_i$ acts simultaneously as a query (asking "who is relevant to me?") and contributes a key and value (answering "here is what I offer to others"). This lets the model build **context-dependent representations**: the embedding of the word *it* can depend on whether the surrounding sentence is about an animal or a street.

Because there are no recurrences, all $n$ outputs $\boldsymbol{y}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{y}_n$ can be computed in parallel — this is what makes Transformers so much faster to train than RNNs.

---

## Summary

The full derivation in one view:

$$
\underbrace{f(\boldsymbol{x}) = \sum_i \alpha_i(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_{1:n})\, y_i}_{\text{kernel regression}}
\;\xrightarrow{\text{Gaussian kernel}}\;
\underbrace{\sum_i \operatorname{softmax}_i\!\left(-\tfrac{\beta^2}{2}\|\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_i\|^2\right) y_i}_{\text{nonparametric attention}}
$$

$$
\xrightarrow{\text{generalize similarity, rename}}\;
\underbrace{\sum_i \operatorname{softmax}_i\!\left(a(\boldsymbol{q}, \boldsymbol{k}_i)\right) \boldsymbol{v}_i}_{\text{general attention}}
\;\xrightarrow{\text{expand, drop constants}}\;
\underbrace{\operatorname{softmax}\!\left(\tfrac{Q K^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right) V}_{\text{scaled dot-product}}
\;\xrightarrow{\text{learned projections + self-query}}\;
\underbrace{\boldsymbol{y}_i = \operatorname{Attn}(W^{(q)} \boldsymbol{x}_i, W^{(k)} X, W^{(v)} X)}_{\text{self-attention}}
$$

Self-attention is not a mysterious black box. It is the natural result of applying a Gaussian similarity kernel, normalizing with softmax, generalizing the similarity, adding learned projections, and letting a sequence attend to itself. Every piece has a reason.
